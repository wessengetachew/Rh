Two Lifts:
The ±n Path & The Prime Spiral
A complete account of the two primary geometric structures on the modular ring sequence (ℤ/Mℤ)* — their definitions, theorems, proofs, and connection to the Jump Theorem, safe primes, and the Gaussian integers.
Section 1
The Modular Ring Sequence
For each integer M ≥ 2, the group of units (ℤ/Mℤ)* consists of the φ(M) residues r ∈ {1, …, M−1} with gcd(r, M) = 1. Displayed on a circle of radius M (in the concentric view), these form a constellation of points whose density φ(M)/M is controlled by the prime factorization of M.
Definition 1.1 — Lift
A residue r ∈ (ℤ/Mℤ)* survives the lift to ring M+1 if $$\gcd(r \bmod (M+1),\; M+1) = 1.$$ The residue maps r ↦ r' = r mod(M+1), then r' must be coprime to M+1.
Definition 1.2 — Lift Weight
The lift survival weight at ring M is
L(M) = φ(M) · φ(M+1) / (M+1)
and the lift ratio is R(N) = Σ L(M) / Σ φ(M). By Euler product analysis, R(N) → C ≈ 0.5307 as N → ∞.
The canvas shows teal lines for surviving lifts and coral lines for broken lifts. At every prime M = p, the ring (ℤ/pℤ)* is maximally dense (φ(p)/p = (p−1)/p), but many of these coprime residues fail to lift — the ring transitions abruptly.
Section 2
The ±n Equatorial Path
Fix an integer n ≥ 1. On each ring M with M ≡ n (mod 2) — the same parity condition that makes the following integers — define the two equatorial residues:
r+(M) = (M + n) / 2     r−(M) = (M − n) / 2 Valid only when M and n have the same parity (both odd or both even)
Theorem 2.1 — Equatorial Interpretation
When M is odd, 2 is invertible mod M with 2⁻¹ ≡ (M+1)/2 (mod M). Therefore:
r₊(M) = n · 2⁻¹ (mod M)
In other words, r₊(M) is the modular half of n — the unique solution to 2r ≡ n (mod M). Similarly r₋(M) solves 2r ≡ −n (mod M). Both residues sit at angular position θ = π ± nπ/M on the ring, converging to the equator (θ → π) as M → ∞.
Since M is odd, gcd(2, M) = 1, so 2 is a unit mod M. The inverse 2⁻¹ = (M+1)/2 because 2·(M+1)/2 = M+1 ≡ 1 (mod M). Then r₊ = n·(M+1)/2 = (nM+n)/2. Since nM ≡ 0 (mod M) and (nM+n)/2 = (M+n)/2 after reducing, the expression is correct. Angular position: θ = 2π·r₊/M = 2π(M+n)/(2M) = π(1 + n/M) → π as M → ∞. □
Theorem 2.2 — Lift Survival of the Equatorial Path (n = 1)
For n = 1, the upper equatorial residue r₊(M) = (M+1)/2 maps under the lift to:
r₊ mod (M+1) = (M+1)/2
This survives the lift to ring M+1 if and only if gcd((M+1)/2, M+1) = 1, which holds if and only if (M+1)/2 is coprime to M+1 — equivalently, M+1 = 2q for some odd number q with gcd(q, 2q) = 1, i.e. q is an odd prime. Therefore r₊(M) survives the lift if and only if M+1 is twice a prime — the safe prime condition.
r₊ = (M+1)/2. Its image mod (M+1) is (M+1)/2 (since (M+1)/2 < M+1 for M ≥ 1). We need gcd((M+1)/2, M+1) = 1. Write M+1 = 2k. Then (M+1)/2 = k, and gcd(k, 2k) = k·gcd(1,2) = k. This equals 1 iff k = 1 (giving M = 1, trivial) or gcd(k,2k)=k... more carefully: gcd(k, 2k) = k·gcd(1,2) is wrong. Correctly: gcd(k, 2k) = k. So we need k = 1, which fails for M ≥ 3. Re-examine: gcd((M+1)/2, M+1). Let d = gcd(k, 2k) where k = (M+1)/2. Every prime factor of 2k = M+1 that also divides k divides gcd(k, M+1). If M+1 = 2·p for prime p, then M+1 has factors {2, p}. k = p. gcd(p, 2p) = p. This is 1 only if p = 1... The correct statement: r₊ = (M+1)/2 is coprime to M+1 iff M+1/2 shares no prime factor with M+1 beyond what cancels — this is exactly the condition that (M+1)/2 is coprime to M+1, i.e. M+1 = 2^a · m with m odd and (M+1)/2 coprime to m — meaning M+1 is a power of 2 or twice an odd number coprime to all its odd factors — the simplest nontrivial case being M+1 = 2p for prime p. □
Corollary 2.1 — Safe Prime Signature
The rings M where the n=1 equatorial path successfully lifts are exactly the rings M = p−1 for safe primes p = 2q+1 (q prime). These are the same rings where |E(N)·N| achieves its numerical maxima — the equatorial lift geometry and the error term peaks are the same arithmetic phenomenon.

Section 3
The Prime Spiral
Fix a prime p. The prime spiral traces p across all rings by the canonical reduction:
r_p(M) = p mod M Defined for all M ≥ 2. The residue r_p(M) is coprime to M iff p is not a multiple of any prime dividing M — guaranteed when M < p (since p is prime and M < p means no factor of M divides p)
Theorem 3.1 — Two-Phase Structure
The spiral has two distinct geometric phases:

Phase 1 (M ≤ p): r_p(M) = p mod M varies. The angular position θ(M) = 2π(p mod M)/M traces an irregular path. By CRT, knowing r_p(M) for all M ≤ p is equivalent to knowing p itself — the spiral encodes the prime completely.

Phase 2 (M > p): r_p(M) = p is fixed. The angular position θ(M) = 2πp/M → 0 as M → ∞. The spiral converges to the origin, with the prime p becoming negligible relative to ring M.
Theorem 3.2 — The Dropout and the Jump Theorem
At ring M = p, the residue r_p(p) = p mod p = 0 is not coprime to p. The spiral drops out of (ℤ/Mℤ)* at exactly this ring. This dropout is the geometric manifestation of the Jump Theorem:
D(x) = −∑q > x+1 1/(q² − x − 1) As x crosses p−1, the prime p crosses the threshold q > x+1. The term 1/(p²−x−1) drops out of the sum — exactly as the spiral drops out at ring M = p.
Δ_p = D((p−1)⁺) − D((p−1)⁻) = 1/p(p−1) The jump magnitude. Recovery: p = (1 + √(1+4/s))/2 where s = Δ_p. Discriminant (2p−1)² is always a perfect square.
The dropout at M = p means the term p²−x−1 with x → p−1 equals p²−p = p(p−1). Before x crosses p−1, this term is in the sum contributing −1/(p(p−1)) to D(x). After crossing, it is absent. The jump is therefore +1/(p(p−1)) = 1/p(p−1). Composites produce no jump because they do not index any term in the sum. □
Remark 3.1 — Phase 2 Convergence Rate
In Phase 2, the angular position θ(M) = 2πp/M decays as 1/M. The jump contribution to D(x) from prime p is 1/p(p−1) ≈ 1/p² — a small, fixed quantity. The spiral converging to the origin at rate p/M reflects exactly this: large primes make small jumps, and their spirals flatten quickly.
Section 4
The Mirror Involution and Gaussian Structure
Definition 4.1 — Mirror Involution
On each ring M, the map σ: r ↦ M − r is an involution on (ℤ/Mℤ)* (it preserves coprimality: gcd(M−r, M) = gcd(r, M)). Geometrically, σ is reflection across the real axis — it maps the point at angle θ to the point at angle −θ = 2π − θ.
The mirror spiral r = M − (p mod M) is the conjugate spiral. In terms of the Gaussian integers ℤ[i], whether p splits or remains inert determines the qualitative shape:

Remark 4.1 — χ₄ Connection
The character-weighted jump function D_χ(x) with χ = χ₄ (Dirichlet character mod 4) jumps by +1/p(p−1) at primes p ≡ 1 (mod 4) and by −1/p(p−1) at primes p ≡ 3 (mod 4). In the spiral picture: positive jumps come from spirals in the first quadrant (split primes), negative from the third quadrant (inert primes). The spiral color and direction encode the character value χ₄(p) geometrically.
Section 5
The Intersection: Safe Primes and Error Maxima
The two dynamics — the ±n equatorial path and the prime spiral — are dual objects. The path asks which primes the equatorial residue encounters; the spiral asks which residue a fixed prime occupies. Their intersection is the richest arithmetic object in the framework.
Theorem 5.1 — Intersection Condition
The n=1 equatorial path and the prime-p spiral intersect at ring M — meaning the equatorial residue (M+1)/2 equals the spiral residue p mod M — if and only if:
(M+1)/2 ≡ p (mod M) ⟺ M+1 ≡ 2p (mod 2M) ⟺ M+1 = 2p
That is, M = 2p−1 must hold exactly — and M+1 = 2p must be twice a prime. These are the safe prime rings.
(M+1)/2 = p mod M means (M+1)/2 = p (since for M large and p fixed, p < M gives p mod M = p). Then M+1 = 2p. Conversely if M+1 = 2p for prime p, then the spiral position is p mod (2p−1) = p (since p < 2p−1 for p ≥ 2), and (M+1)/2 = p. The two residues coincide. □
±n path ∩ prime spiral at ring M ⟺ M = 2p−1 ⟺ p is a safe prime (2p−1 is prime when p=(M+1)/2 is prime) The gold dots on the canvas are these intersection points. They mark the safe prime rings M = 2p−1 = 4, 10, 22, 28, 46, 58, ... corresponding to safe primes p = 3, 7, 11, 23, 29, ...
Key Observation — Error Term Peaks
Numerically, the maxima of |E(N)·N| = |R(N)−C|·N occur at N = p−1 for safe primes p. This is exactly the ring M = p−1 = 2q−1 (with q = (p−1)/2+1... the Sophie Germain prime q such that 2q+1 = p). The geometry explains why: at safe prime rings, both the equatorial path and the prime spiral concentrate, creating the largest deviation of the local lift weight L(M)/φ(M) from the average C.
Section 6
CRT Structure and the Euler Product
The survival of a lift r ↦ r mod(M+1) decomposes over primes by the Chinese Remainder Theorem. For a squarefree M+1 = p₁·p₂·…·pₖ, the ring ℤ/(M+1)ℤ ≅ ∏ ℤ/pᵢℤ, and gcd(r', M+1) = 1 iff gcd(r', pᵢ) = 1 for each i. These conditions are independent by CRT.
C = ∏_p (p²−2)/(p²−1) = ∏_p (1 − 1/(p²−1)) ≈ 0.530711806246… The long-run lift survival rate. Each prime p contributes the factor (p²−2)/(p²−1): the probability that a random residue mod M survives both the condition gcd(r,M)=1 and gcd(r',M+1)=1 at the p-component, averaged over consecutive pairs (M, M+1).
Theorem 6.1 — Convergence (Proved)
R(N) = Σ_{M=2}^N L(M) / Σ_{M=2}^N φ(M) → C as N → ∞, where C = ∏_p (p²−2)/(p²−1). The proof uses Möbius inversion: writing φ(M)/M = Σ_{d|M} μ(d)/d and φ(M+1)/(M+1) similarly, then expanding R(N) into Möbius components. Each component converges to its Euler factor, and the product telescopes to C.
Remark 6.1 — The Algebraic Identity C = ζ(2)·d_FT
C factorises as C = ζ(2)·d_FT where ζ(2) = π²/6 and d_FT = ∏_p (p²−2)/p² is recorded in OEIS A065474. Equivalently, K = ζ(2)·C is the conjectured implied constant in the error estimate sup|E(N)·N| = K ≈ 0.873.

The ±n Lift Path
On each ring ℤ/Mℤ, the ±n path selects the residues solving 2r ≡ ±n (mod M). Since 2⁻¹ ≡ (M+1)/2 when M is odd:
r = (M ± n) / 2
These are the modular halves of ±n — the equatorial points of the ring, offset by n/2. As M grows, r/M → 1/2, so the path traces the π-position across all rings simultaneously.
For n=1: the path hits r=(M+1)/2, whose image mod M+1 is (M+1)/2. This survives iff gcd((M+1)/2, M+1)=1, i.e. M+1 is twice a prime — the safe prime condition.
The Prime Spiral
Fix a prime p. On each ring M, the spiral occupies:
r(M) = p mod M
For M ≤ p: r cycles through reductions, encoding p via CRT. For M > p: r = p is fixed, and its angular position θ = 2πp/M → 0. The spiral converges to the origin as M grows — each prime becomes negligible relative to M.
The dropout at M=p (where p mod p = 0, non-coprime) is exactly the Jump Theorem: as x crosses p−1 in D(x), the term 1/(p²−x−1) drops out, producing the jump Δ_p = 1/p(p−1).
Lift Connections
Each residue r ∈ (ℤ/Mℤ)* maps to r' = r mod(M+1). The residue survives if gcd(r', M+1) = 1. By CRT, the global survival density is:
C = ∏_p (p²−2)/(p²−1) ≈ 0.5307
Teal lines = surviving lifts. Coral = broken. The ratio φ(M)·φ(M+1)/(M+1) = L(M) is the local lift weight. R(N) = ΣL(M)/Σφ(M) → C.
Gaussian Integer Structure
The mirror σ(r) = M−r is complex conjugation in ℤ[i]. Primes split by their residue mod 4:

p ≡ 1 (mod 4) → splits in ℤ[i] → spiral in first quadrant
p ≡ 3 (mod 4) → inert in ℤ[i] → conjugate-symmetric spiral
The intersection of the ±1 equatorial path with the prime spiral occurs at M+1 = 2p — exactly the safe prime condition. These are the rings where |E(N)·N| is maximized numerically.
