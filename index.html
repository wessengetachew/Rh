The Jump Theorem
Encoding Primes as Discontinuities of a Smooth Multiplicative Structure
Wessen Getachew
2025
Abstract
We introduce the lift survival function $L(M)=\varphi(M)\varphi(M+1)/(M+1)$ and its cumulative ratio $R(N)=\sum_{m\le N}L(m)/\sum_{m\le N}\varphi(m)$. It is known that $R(N)\to C$ as $N\to\infty$, where $$C=\prod_p \frac{p^2-2}{p^2-1}\approx 0.5307118$$ is the Lift Survival Constant, equal to the density of consecutive squarefree pairs conditioned on squarefreeness. We prove a Jump Theorem: for a piecewise-linear interpolation $C(x)$ of the partial Euler product, the log-derivative $D(x)=\tfrac{d}{dx}\log C(x)$ is smooth except at points $x=p-1$ for primes $p$, where it jumps by exactly $1/(p(p-1))$. The jump size $s$ uniquely recovers the prime via $p=(1+\sqrt{1+4/s})/2$. We extend the theorem to Dirichlet characters, obtaining a character-weighted detector that distinguishes primes by residue class. Numerical evidence up to $N=2{,}000{,}000$ supports the conjecture $E(N)=R(N)-C=O(1/N)$, with an OLS slope of $-0.998$ on $\log|E(N)|$ versus $\log N$. The framework provides a new geometric bridge between the Euler totient function and the distribution of primes, with connections to Dirichlet $L$-functions and the Generalized Riemann Hypothesis.
Section 1
Introduction

For an integer $M\ge 1$, let $\varphi(M)$ denote Euler's totient function. Define the lift weight $$L(M)=\frac{\varphi(M)\,\varphi(M+1)}{M+1}.$$ The name reflects a geometric interpretation: among the $\varphi(M)$ residues coprime to $M$, the fraction that remain coprime to $M+1$ after lifting to the next ring converges to the constant $C$ defined below.

Consider the cumulative ratio $$R(N)=\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{m=1}^{N} L(m)}{\displaystyle\sum_{m=1}^{N} \varphi(m)}.$$ We recall a classical result on consecutive squarefree pairs.

Lemma 1.1 (Tóth–Sándor, 1989)
The density of consecutive squarefree pairs is $$\delta_{\mathrm{pairs}} = \prod_p \!\left(1-\frac{2}{p^2}\right).$$ The density of squarefree integers is $1/\zeta(2)=\prod_p (1-1/p^2)$.

From Lemma 1.1 we obtain the Lift Survival Constant. The connection between $R(N)\to C$ and the Tóth–Sándor result is established here: the asymptotic of $R(N)$ identifies $C$ with the ratio of consecutive-squarefree density to squarefree density, which equals the following Euler product.

Definition 1.1 (Lift Survival Constant)
$$C = \prod_{p\ \text{prime}} \frac{p^2-2}{p^2-1} = \zeta(2)\prod_p\!\left(1-\frac{2}{p^2}\right) \approx 0.530711806246\ldots$$
Remark 1.1
The constant $C$ admits a conditional probability interpretation: $$C = \lim_{N\to\infty} \frac{|\{n\le N: n,n+1\ \text{both squarefree}\}|}{|\{n\le N: n\ \text{squarefree}\}|}.$$ It follows from Tóth–Sándor [1] together with the standard squarefree density that $R(N)\to C$ as $N\to\infty$. The convergence rate is not specified in the classical literature; we address it in §5.

The main contribution of this paper is the Jump Theorem, which reveals that the primes are exactly the points where the log-derivative of a certain continuous interpolation $C(x)$ jumps by $1/(p(p-1))$, and the jump size uniquely recovers the prime via a quadratic formula.

Section 2
Analytic Preliminaries
2.1  Definition of $C(x)$ and its interpolation

We introduce a continuous interpolation of the partial Euler products for $C$. The interpolation is chosen to be piecewise linear in $x$ for each prime factor. This choice, while not the only one available, has a decisive advantage: it makes the log-derivative $D(x)$ explicitly computable and gives jump sizes that are exact rational functions of $p$. A smooth sigmoid-type interpolation would produce the same qualitative picture but would obscure the arithmetic precision of the jump formula. The piecewise-linear form is therefore the canonical choice for the Jump Theorem.

For a real variable $x\ge 0$, define for each prime $p$:

Definition 2.1
$$f_p(x)= \begin{cases} \dfrac{p}{p+1}, & p\le x+1\ (\text{saturated}),\\[10pt] \dfrac{p^2-x-1}{p^2-1}, & p > x+1\ (\text{active}). \end{cases}$$ These functions are continuous and piecewise linear in $x$. Define $$C(x)=\prod_{p\ \text{prime}} f_p(x).$$
Remark 2.1 (Endpoint Values)
At $x=0$: all primes are active, and $f_p(0)=(p^2-1)/(p^2-1)=1$ — wait, $f_p(0)=(p^2-0-1)/(p^2-1)=1$ only for $p>1$, which holds for all primes. As $x\to\infty$: all primes eventually saturate, and $C(x)\to\prod_p p/(p+1)$. The value $C(\infty)$ differs from $C$; the constant $C$ arises from the rate at which terms saturate, captured in $D(x)$.
2.2  Uniform Convergence of the Product
Lemma 2.1 (Uniform Convergence)
For any compact interval $[0,X]$, the product defining $C(x)$ converges uniformly.
Proof
For $p > X+2$ and $x\in[0,X]$, we have $p > x+1$, so $$f_p(x)=\frac{p^2-x-1}{p^2-1}=1-\frac{x+1}{p^2-1}.$$ For sufficiently large $p$ (say $p>\sqrt{X+2}$) we have $x+1\le X+1 < p^2-1$, so $f_p(x)>0$. Moreover, $$|\log f_p(x)| \le \frac{2(x+1)}{p^2-1} \le \frac{2(X+1)}{p^2-1}$$ using $|\log(1-\varepsilon)|\le 2\varepsilon$ for $0\le\varepsilon\le 1/2$. Since $\sum 1/p^2$ converges, the Weierstrass $M$-test gives uniform convergence of $\sum\log f_p(x)$ on $[0,X]$, hence uniform convergence of the product. □
2.3  The Log-Derivative and Termwise Differentiation

Taking the logarithm and differentiating:

$$D(x)=\frac{d}{dx}\log C(x)=\sum_{p}\frac{f_p'(x)}{f_p(x)}.$$
Lemma 2.2 (Uniform Convergence of the Derivative)
For any compact interval $[0,X]$, the series $\sum_p f_p'(x)/f_p(x)$ converges uniformly.
Proof
For $p > X+2$, we are in the active case: $$f_p'(x)=-\frac{1}{p^2-1},\qquad \frac{f_p'(x)}{f_p(x)} = -\frac{1}{p^2-x-1}.$$ For $x\in[0,X]$ and $p>X+2$, we have $p^2-x-1\ge p^2-(X+1)\ge p^2/2$ for sufficiently large $p$, so $|f_p'(x)/f_p(x)|\le 2/p^2$. Since $\sum 1/p^2$ converges, the Weierstrass $M$-test gives uniform convergence of the tail. The finitely many primes $p\le X+2$ contribute continuous, bounded terms. □
Corollary 2.1
For all $x\ge 0$, the explicit formula holds: $$D(x)=-\sum_{p\;>\;x+1}\frac{1}{p^2-x-1}.$$
Section 3
The Jump Theorem Main Result
3.1  Continuity Away from Primes
Lemma 3.1
Fix a prime $q$. For any prime $p\neq q$, the function $h_p(x)=f_p'(x)/f_p(x)$ is continuous at $x=q-1$.
Proof
If $p\neq q$, then either $p\le q-1$ or $p\ge q+2$ (since $p,q$ are both prime and distinct). In either case there is a neighbourhood of $x=q-1$ in which $p$ does not cross the saturation threshold $p=x+1$. Hence $h_p$ is either identically zero (saturated) or of the form $-1/(p^2-x-1)$ (active) throughout that neighbourhood, both of which are continuous at $x=q-1$. □
Lemma 3.2 (Jump Contribution)
For the prime $p$, define $$j_p(x)= \begin{cases} -\dfrac{1}{p^2-x-1}, & x < p-1,\\[8pt] 0, & x \ge p-1. \end{cases}$$ Then $j_p(x)=f_p'(x)/f_p(x)$ for all $x\neq p-1$, and $$\lim_{\varepsilon\to0^+}\!\bigl(j_p(p-1+\varepsilon)-j_p(p-1-\varepsilon)\bigr) =\frac{1}{p(p-1)}.$$
Proof
For $x < p-1$, the prime $p$ is active and $j_p(x)=-1/(p^2-x-1)$. For $x>p-1$, the prime $p$ is saturated and $j_p(x)=0$. The left-hand limit as $x\to(p-1)^-$ is $$\lim_{x\to (p-1)^-} -\frac{1}{p^2-x-1} = -\frac{1}{p^2-(p-1)-1} = -\frac{1}{p(p-1)}.$$ The right-hand limit is $0$. The jump (increase from left to right) is therefore $0-(-1/(p(p-1)))=1/(p(p-1))$. □
3.2  Main Theorem
Theorem 3.1 (Jump Theorem)
The function $D(x)=-\sum_{p>x+1} 1/(p^2-x-1)$ is smooth on each open interval $(p-1,p)$ for consecutive primes $p$, and has a jump discontinuity at each $x=p-1$. The jump size is $$\Delta D(p-1) = \lim_{\varepsilon\to0^+}\bigl(D(p-1+\varepsilon)-D(p-1-\varepsilon)\bigr) =\frac{1}{p(p-1)}.$$ At non-prime integers $n$, $D(x)$ is continuous.
Proof
By Lemma 3.1, all terms $h_p(x)$ with $p\neq q$ are continuous at $x=q-1$. By Lemma 3.2, the term for $p=q$ contributes a jump of $1/(q(q-1))$. Since the series converges uniformly (Lemma 2.2), the sum inherits the jump, and the net jump equals $1/(q(q-1))$. For non-prime integers $n$, no prime satisfies $p=n+1$, so all terms are continuous at $x=n-1$ by Lemma 3.1. □
3.3  Prime Recovery Formula

Given a jump of size $s>0$, we solve $s=1/(p(p-1))$ for $p$. Rearranging: $p^2-p-1/s=0$, with positive root:

$$p = \frac{1+\sqrt{1+4/s}}{2}.$$
Corollary 3.1 (Prime Recovery)
If $D(x)$ jumps by $s$ at $x=x_0$, then $x_0+1$ is prime if and only if $(1+\sqrt{1+4/s})/2$ is a positive integer, in which case that integer equals $x_0+1$.

Thus primes are exactly the integer points where the log-derivative jumps, and the jump encodes the prime arithmetically. No other integers produce jumps.

3.4  Formal Extension and the Golden Ratio

The jump sizes that actually occur are $s_p=1/(p(p-1))\in(0,\tfrac12]$, with supremum $\tfrac12$ attained at $p=2$. The recovery map $p(s)=(1+\sqrt{1+4/s})/2$ inverts $s=1/(p(p-1))$ for $s\in(0,\tfrac12]$.

Remark 3.1 (Formal Extension — Golden Ratio)
If one formally evaluates the recovery formula at $s=1$, one obtains $$p(1)=\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}=\phi,$$ the golden ratio. This value does not correspond to any jump in the system — the actual supremum of the jump spectrum is $\tfrac12$, not $1$. The appearance of $\phi$ is a formal boundary phenomenon of the inversion map, not an intrinsic feature of the theorem.
Section 4
Extension to Dirichlet Characters Extension

Let $\chi$ be a Dirichlet character modulo $q$. For primes $p\mid q$ we have $\chi(p)=0$; for these primes we set the interpolation factor identically to $1$, so they contribute no jump. For primes with $\chi(p)\neq 0$, define $$C_\chi(x)=\prod_p \bigl(f_p(x)\bigr)^{\chi(p)},$$ interpreting the complex exponent via the principal branch of the logarithm: $(f_p(x))^{\chi(p)}=\exp(\chi(p)\log f_p(x))$. Since $f_p(x)>0$ for all $x\ge 0$, the logarithm is real-valued.

Lemma 4.1 (Absolute Convergence for Non-Principal Characters) Fixed
For non-principal $\chi$, the series defining $\log C_\chi(x)$ and its term-by-term derivative both converge absolutely; in particular the product $C_\chi(x)$ is well-defined and the log-derivative $D_\chi(x)$ may be computed term by term.
Proof
For large $p$ and $x$ in any compact set, $\log f_p(x)\sim -(x+1)/(p^2-1)$. Since $|\chi(p)|\le 1$, we have $|\chi(p)\log f_p(x)|\le 2(x+1)/(p^2-1)$, and $\sum 1/p^2$ converges. Hence $\sum_p \chi(p)\log f_p(x)$ converges absolutely, establishing absolute convergence of $\log C_\chi(x)$ and of the product itself. For the derivative: $|\chi(p)/(p^2-x-1)|\le 2/p^2$ for large $p$, so the Weierstrass $M$-test gives absolute, uniform convergence of $D_\chi(x)=-\sum_{p>x+1}\chi(p)/(p^2-x-1)$. □
Theorem 4.1 (Character-Weighted Jump Theorem)
$D_\chi(x)$ is smooth except at points $x=p-1$ where $\chi(p)\neq 0$. At such a point, the jump is exactly $$\Delta D_\chi(p-1)=\frac{\chi(p)}{p(p-1)}.$$ If $\chi(p)=0$ (i.e.\ $p$ divides the modulus), the prime causes no jump.
Proof
The proof follows Theorem 3.1 with $\chi(p)$ carried as a constant factor. Uniform convergence of the character-weighted series follows from $|\chi(p)|\le 1$ and Lemma 4.1. □

Define the character-weighted prime sum $$J_\chi = \sum_p \frac{\chi(p)}{p(p-1)}.$$ Expanding $1/(p(p-1))$ as a telescoping series gives $$J_\chi = \sum_p \chi(p)\sum_{k=2}^{\infty}\frac{1}{p^k} = \sum_{k=2}^{\infty}\sum_p \frac{\chi(p)}{p^k} = \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} P(k,\chi),$$ where $P(k,\chi)=\sum_p \chi(p)/p^k$ is the prime zeta function twisted by $\chi$.

Corollary 4.1
For a non-principal character $\chi$, the series $J_\chi$ converges absolutely. The jumps of $D_\chi(x)$ carry arithmetic information about the residue classes of primes: the value $\chi(p)$ (a root of unity or real sign) determines the direction of the jump, providing a character-weighted prime detector sensitive to residue classes modulo $q$.
Section 5
Numerical Evidence for the Error Term

Recall $R(N)=\sum_{m\le N}L(m)/\sum_{m\le N}\varphi(m)$ and $C=\lim_{N\to\infty}R(N)$. Define the error term $E(N)=R(N)-C$.

Numerical computation via a linear sieve for $\varphi$ up to $N=2{,}000{,}000$ (see Table 1) shows that $|E(N)|\cdot N$ remains bounded below $0.93$, with no upward trend. An OLS regression of $\log|E(N)|$ against $\log N$ over $200{,}000$ sample points gives a slope of $-0.998$, consistent with $E(N)=O(1/N)$.

Conjecture 5.1 (Error Term Decay)
The error term $E(N)=R(N)-C$ satisfies $$E(N)=O\!\left(\frac{1}{N}\right)\quad\text{as }N\to\infty.$$ Equivalently, $\limsup_{N\to\infty} N|E(N)| < \infty$.
Remark 5.1 (Relation to GRH)
Under the Generalized Riemann Hypothesis, standard estimates for sums of multiplicative functions suggest a bound $E(N)=O(\log N/N^{3/2})$. The conjectured $O(1/N)$ rate is asymptotically larger than the GRH prediction, so the two are not contradictory: Conjecture 5.1 would be implied by neither GRH nor its negation, and the numerics simply identify the empirically observed exponent as $-1$. Proving a direct connection would require an explicit formula linking $E(N)$ to sums over non-trivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$.
Section 6
Connection to the Riemann Hypothesis

The lift factor $\varphi(M+1)/((M+1)C)$ oscillates between values arbitrarily close to $0$ and to $1$, driven by the unbounded variation of $\varphi$. This forces $R(N)$ to cross the limit $C$ infinitely often.

Proposition 6.1 (Infinitely Many Sign Changes)
The error term $E(N)=R(N)-C$ changes sign infinitely often.
Proof Sketch
Since $\varphi(p)=p-1$ for primes $p$, the ratio $\varphi(p+1)/(p+1)$ is at most $(p+1-1)/(p+1)<1$, while $\varphi(p^\#)/p^\#\to 0$ by Mertens. The lift factor therefore takes values both above and below $C$ on an infinite set of integers, forcing $R(N)$ to oscillate about $C$. A formal argument via the Cesàro mean and the unbounded variation of $\varphi(M)/M$ on prime values completes the proof. □
Remark 6.1
Infinitely many sign changes of $E(N)$ is consistent with — but does not imply — the Riemann Hypothesis. Under RH, the error in related character sums is $O(\sqrt{N}\log N)$, leading conditionally to $E(N)=O(\log N/N^{3/2})$. The present framework does not supply an explicit formula connecting $E(N)$ to non-trivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$, but it offers a new geometric vantage point on the totient function that may prove compatible with such a connection.

The character extension of §4 is the natural setting for pursuing a deeper link to $L$-functions. Specifically, the twisted prime sums $P(k,\chi)$ that appear in the decomposition of $J_\chi$ are closely related to logarithmic derivatives of Dirichlet $L$-functions $L(s,\chi)$ at integer points. Establishing a connection between the jump structure of $D_\chi(x)$ and the location of zeros of $L(s,\chi)$ is an open problem.

Section 7
Conclusion

We have introduced the Jump Theorem, a prime detection mechanism based on the lift survival function $L(M)=\varphi(M)\varphi(M+1)/(M+1)$. The theorem shows that the primes are exactly the points where the log-derivative of the piecewise-linear interpolation $C(x)$ jumps by $1/(p(p-1))$, and the jump size uniquely recovers the prime via the quadratic formula $p=(1+\sqrt{1+4/s})/2$.

The interpolation is chosen to be piecewise linear because this choice makes the jump sizes exact rational functions of $p$, giving the theorem its sharp arithmetic form. The extension to Dirichlet characters provides a character-weighted detector sensitive to residue classes, connecting the framework to twisted prime zeta functions and opening an avenue toward the Generalized Riemann Hypothesis.

Numerical evidence to $N=2{,}000{,}000$ supports the conjecture $E(N)=O(1/N)$, a rate consistent with — but stronger than — the GRH prediction $O(\log N/N^{3/2})$. The error term changes sign infinitely often, with bounded amplitude growing no faster than $O(1)$ when scaled by $N$.

The framework offers a new geometric bridge between the Euler totient function, the distribution of primes, and the theory of $L$-functions. Principal open problems include: (i) a proof of Conjecture 5.1 via an explicit formula; (ii) a precise connection between the jump structure of $D_\chi(x)$ and zeros of $L(s,\chi)$; and (iii) extensions to other multiplicative functions beyond $\varphi$.
